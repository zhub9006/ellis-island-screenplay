# Screenplay Outline — Version 1

## Title: (Working Title — TBD)

**Setting:** January 1905, Southern Italy → Atlantic Crossing → Ellis Island → Little Italy, Manhattan

## Three-Act Structure

### ACT I — The Departure (Southern Italy)

**Scene 1:** The village in Campania (or Calabria/Sicily). A family decision to emigrate. The father has received word from a paesano already in New York — there is work. The mother is torn. The children don't fully understand.

**Scene 2:** The train to Naples. The landscape of southern Italy passing by — the hills, the poverty, the beauty. The father counting their limited money.

**Scene 3:** Naples port. The chaos of the emigration process. The shipping clerk writing their names on the manifest — misspelling them. The steerage ticket purchased. The last look at Italian soil.

### ACT II — The Crossing & Ellis Island

**Scene 4:** Life in steerage. The cramped bunks, the smells, the seasickness. The mother trying to cook with limited provisions. The children playing despite everything. The father talking with other paesani about what awaits them.

**Scene 5:** Arrival in New York Harbor. January 15, 1905 — gray, cold, icy. The ship anchors. The barge comes alongside. The family bundles up and steps onto the open deck of the ferry. The wind. The ice in the water. Ellis Island growing larger through the mist.

**Scene 6:** Ellis Island processing. The staircase. The chalk marks. The terror of the medical inspection. The interpreter asking questions in their dialect. The relief of passing through. The view of Manhattan through the Great Hall windows.

**Scene 7:** The ferry to Manhattan. Stepping onto the Battery. The noise, the cold, the overwhelming scale of the city. A paesano waiting to meet them — the connection that will bring them to Little Italy.

### ACT III — Little Italy

**Scene 8:** The tenement on Mulberry Street. The cramped apartment. The airshaft. The neighbors — all Italian, all from different regions, speaking different dialects. The mother trying to make the space feel like home.

**Scene 9:** The father's work. Construction labor, dock work, or padrone arrangement. The physical toll. The exploitation. The pride of earning American money.

**Scene 10:** The mother's work. Piecework at home. Taking in boarders. The children sent to work or school. The daily struggle to survive and maintain dignity.

**Scene 11:** A crisis. Illness, a Black Hand threat, a conflict with neighbors, or a problem at work. The family must navigate American systems they don't understand.

**Scene 12:** Resolution. A moment of hope — a feast day celebration, a child succeeding in school, a small business started, a letter from home. The family is not yet thriving, but they are surviving, and they are beginning to belong.
