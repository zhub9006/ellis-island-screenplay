## Screenwriting Implications

1. **Short Winter Days:** Only ~9½ hours of daylight. Ships arriving in the morning would see the harbor in dim, cold light. Immigrants disembarking in late afternoon would face near-dark conditions — a powerful visual metaphor for entering an unknown world.

2. **Waxing Crescent Moon (18% illumination):** A sliver of light in the sky — symbolically perfect for a story about hope emerging from darkness. The moon would be visible during daytime (rising at 10 AM), setting after sunset, offering a thin silver line over the harbor at dusk.

3. **Early Sunset (4:52 PM):** By the time immigrants finished processing at Ellis Island (which could take hours), it would be dark. The ferry to Manhattan would cross the harbor under night skies with the crescent moon low — an unforgettable cinematic moment.

4. **Sunrise Timing:** Ships entering New York Harbor at dawn would see the Statue of Liberty emerge from morning mist as the sun climbed — the classic "first sight of America" moment, historically recounted by many Italian immigrants.

## Seasonal Notes for January

- Sun arc is low in the southern sky all day
- Long shadows even at midday
- Dawn colors (pink/gold) appear around 6:45–7:00 AM
- Twilight ends ~5:20 PM; full darkness by 5:30 PM
- These conditions are essentially identical for January 1905 (within ±1–2 minutes variance)
