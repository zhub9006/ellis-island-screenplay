# Italian Immigrants in New York, circa 1905

## Cultural History & Daily Life Research Notes

### Immigration Numbers

- Between 1900 and 1915, **3 million Italians** immigrated to the United States
- In 1905 alone, approximately **200,000+ Italians** arrived at Ellis Island
- Italians were the **single largest immigrant group** during this period
- Most came from southern Italy and Sicily (Campania, Calabria, Sicily, Abruzzi) — regions suffering from poverty, agricultural crisis, and lack of opportunity

### The Journey

- **Steamship crossing:** Typically 7–14 days depending on the ship and route
- **Steerage class:** The cheapest passage ($25–30); cramped quarters below deck, shared bunks, minimal sanitation, poor food
- **Common shipping lines:** Hamburg-America Line, North German Lloyd, Italian Line (Navigazione Generale Italiana), Fabre Line
- **Ports of departure:** Naples, Palermo, Genoa — Naples was the primary port for southern Italians
- **Luggage:** Most brought only a single trunk or bundle — clothing, some food, a few personal items, perhaps a religious medal or family photograph

### Ellis Island Processing (1905)

Based on NPS and History.com sources:

- **Arrival:** Ships anchored in the harbor; immigrants ferried to Ellis Island on government barges
- **Tagging:** Each immigrant was tagged with a number from the ship's manifest
- **Medical inspection:** Quick "six-second" medical exam — doctors watched immigrants climb stairs, checking for signs of illness, lameness, or mental deficiency. Those flagged were marked with chalk symbols on their clothing ("X" for mental issues, "K" for hernia, etc.)
- **Legal inspection:** Name, occupation, destination, ability to work, criminal record check
- **Duration:** Most passed through in **3–7 hours**; some detained for days or weeks
- **Rejection rate:** Only about **2%** were deported — most for medical reasons
- **Name changes:** Contrary to myth, names were NOT systematically changed at Ellis Island. Officials used the ship's manifest names. However, some immigrants voluntarily simplified their names later.

### Settlement in New York

#### Little Italy (Mulberry Street Area)

- **Location:** Lower Manhattan, centered on Mulberry Street between Canal and Bleecker
- **Population:** By 1905, approximately **30,000–40,000** Italians lived in this neighborhood
- **Housing:** Tenement buildings — 4–6 story cramped apartments, often without hot water, proper ventilation, or indoor plumbing
- **Typical apartment:** 2–3 rooms for a family of 5–8 people; rent approximately **$8–12/month**
- **Airshaft:** Tenements were built around narrow airshafts that provided minimal light and ventilation

#### Daily Life

- **Work:** Men typically worked as laborers — construction, dock work, street cleaning, subway excavation, or in small trades. Many were **padrone** (labor broker) recruits who were essentially indentured workers
- **Women's work:** Homework — piecework sewing, embroidery, making artificial flowers; some worked in garment factories; many took in boarders to supplement income
- **Children:** Often worked too — as newsboys, shoeshine boys, or in factories. School attendance was inconsistent
- **Food:** Southern Italian cuisine adapted to American ingredients — pasta, bread, olive oil (when affordable), vegetables, some meat on Sundays. Street vendors sold produce, fish, and roasted chestnuts
- **Social life:** Centered on the **paesani** (people from the same hometown) — immigrants organized by village of origin, not just by nationality
- **Religion:** Catholic — local parish churches were central to community life; feast days (especially the Feast of San Gennaro, begun in 1926 but smaller celebrations existed earlier) were important
- **Language:** Most spoke **dialects** (Neapolitan, Sicilian, Calabrese) rather than standard Italian. This created barriers even among different Italian groups

#### The Padrone System

- A controversial labor arrangement where a **padrone** (boss/middleman) recruited Italian workers, arranged their passage, found them jobs, and took a significant cut of their wages
- Some padroni were exploitative; others provided a genuine service helping immigrants navigate an unfamiliar country
- By 1905, the system was being investigated and criticized by social reformers

#### Challenges & Prejudice

- **Discrimination:** Italians faced significant prejudice — portrayed in newspapers as ignorant, prone to crime, and racially inferior (some Americans classified them as "non-white")
- **Crime stereotype:** The "Black Hand" (La Mano Nera) — a form of extortion — was sensationalized in newspapers, though it affected only a tiny fraction of the community
- **Health concerns:** Italians were sometimes blamed for spreading disease; tenement conditions made public health a real concern
- **Language barriers:** Dialect speakers struggled with English and even with standard Italian
- **North vs. South tension:** Northern Italians often looked down on southern Italians; this divide persisted in America

### Cultural Elements for the Screenplay

#### Authentic Details to Include

1. **The ship's manifest:** Families were listed by number; names were spelled as the shipping clerk recorded them (often incorrectly)
2. **The chalk marks:** Medical inspectors used chalk letters on clothing — a terrifying moment for immigrants who didn't understand the symbols
3. **The food at Ellis Island:** Immigrants were fed meals while waiting — the food was often unfamiliar and unappetizing to Italians
4. **The money exchange:** Immigrants exchanged foreign currency at teller windows on Ellis Island
5. **The railroad ticket counter:** Those not staying in NYC bought train tickets to destinations across America
6. **The staircase:** The famous staircase leading up to the Registry Room — immigrants climbed it under the watchful eyes of medical inspectors
7. **The view from the island:** Through the windows of the Great Hall, immigrants could see the Manhattan skyline — the towers and smokestacks of the city they'd heard stories about

#### Sensory Details

- **Smells:** Steerage (garlic, vomit, damp wool, coal smoke); Ellis Island (disinfectant, food, body odor, wet clothing); Little Italy (roasted chestnuts, bread baking, fish from street vendors, sewage in tenement airshafts)
- **Sounds:** The harbor (fog horns, ship engines, barge engines, wind); Ellis Island (shouting interpreters, crying children, clattering baggage, multiple languages); Little Italy (Italian dialects shouted across streets, horse hooves on cobblestones, street vendor cries, children playing in gutters)
- **Visuals:** Gray harbor light; the vast, tiled Great Hall with its high arched ceiling; the dense, narrow streets of Mulberry Bend; laundry strung between tenement buildings; the glow of gas lamps at night
