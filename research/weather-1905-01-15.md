# Historical Weather: New York City, January 15, 1905

## Arrival Scene Weather Research

### Source Limitations

The WeatherAPI historical data service only covers dates from 2010 onward, so direct API retrieval for 1905 was not possible. The following is compiled from available historical climate records, NOAA archives, and newspaper accounts.

### January 1905 — General Winter Context

New York City winters in the early 1900s were notably harsh, especially for immigrants arriving by ship across the Atlantic. Key facts:

- **Average January Temperature (1900–1905 era):** ~28–32°F (−2 to 0°C)
- **Typical Conditions:** Cold, often overcast; frequent snowfall; biting winds off the harbor
- **Harbor Conditions:** Ice frequently formed in the Upper Bay and along the Hudson; ferry boats sometimes struggled through ice
- **Wind:** Prevailing northwest winds brought cold air from Canada; gusts across the open harbor were brutal for passengers on deck

### January 15, 1905 — Specific Research Notes

Based on historical newspaper accounts and NOAA climate data for the NYC region:

- **Temperature:** Likely in the range of **25–35°F (−4 to 2°C)**. January 1905 was part of a notably cold winter stretch.
- **Sky:** Overcast or partly cloudy — winter in NYC was frequently gray and gloomy
- **Wind:** Moderate to brisk northwest winds (10–20 mph), making the harbor feel much colder
- **Precipitation:** Light snow or flurries possible; January was a snowy month in NYC during this era
- **Harbor Visibility:** Limited — fog and mist were common in winter mornings over the harbor
- **Water:** Partially frozen edges in the harbor; ships' wakes could carry ice chunks

### Dramatic Implications for the Screenplay

1. **The Crossing:** Passengers on steamships would have endured freezing conditions on deck. Many Italian immigrants traveled in steerage — the cheapest, lowest deck — with minimal heating and ventilation. Arriving in cold harbor air after days at sea would be physically punishing.

2. **The Ferry Ride:** Immigrants were ferried from the steamship to Ellis Island on small government barges. In winter, this short ride across the icy harbor would be bitterly cold, with wind cutting across the open water.

3. **The Great Hall:** Once inside Ellis Island's Registry Room (Great Hall), immigrants would finally feel warmth from the building's heating system. The contrast between the freezing harbor and the cavernous, heated hall would be dramatic — steam rising from wet clothing, the smell of damp wool and sea salt.

4. **Exiting to Manhattan:** After processing, immigrants took the ferry to Manhattan. Emerging onto the Battery or into the streets of lower Manhattan in January meant facing cold cobblestone streets, horse-drawn carriages, and the noise of a city in winter.

### Recommended Scene Weather Description

> *The morning of January 15, 1905 broke gray and cold over New York Harbor. A thin layer of ice clung to the pilings along the Battery. The steamship lay at anchor, her hull streaked with salt and frost, and the barge that would carry the immigrants to Ellis Island churned through water that carried chunks of harbor ice. The wind — a northwest gale that had followed them across the Atlantic — still bit at exposed skin. Inside the ship, in the steerage compartments below, families huddled in damp wool coats, the air thick with the smell of garlic, olive oil, and seasickness. When the barge finally pulled away from the ship's side, the immigrants standing on its open deck turned their faces away from the wind and watched the low, brick buildings of Ellis Island grow larger through the mist.*
