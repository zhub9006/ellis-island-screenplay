## The Great Arrival: Context & Scale

### Numbers
- 1880s: ~300,000 Italian immigrants arrived
- 1890s: ~600,000
- 1900–1910: **over 2 million** (the peak decade)
- By 1920: ~4 million Italians had emigrated to the U.S.
- In 1905 alone, hundreds of thousands passed through Ellis Island

### Why They Left Italy
- **Economic hardship:** Southern Italy (Mezzogiorno) was deeply impoverished — subsistence farming, landlessness, feudal-like tenant systems
- **Political instability:** Post-unification Italy (1861) left southerners marginalized; the government favored northern industrialization
- **Natural disasters:** Earthquakes, crop failures, malaria endemic in southern regions
- **Overpopulation:** Rural villages couldn't sustain growing families
- **"Birds of Passage":** Many intended to work temporarily in America, earn money, and return — but most stayed

### Regional Origins
- **Predominantly southern:** Sicily, Calabria, Campania, Basilicata, Puglia
- **Northern Italians** arrived too (Lombardy, Veneto) but were fewer and often more skilled
- This regional divide created class tensions even within Italian-American communities

## The Journey: Steerage to Ellis Island

### The Transatlantic Voyage
- **Duration:** 10–14 days from Naples or Genoa to New York
- **Conditions:** Steerage class was cramped, unsanitary, and terrifying
  - Hundreds packed into lower decks
  - Shared toilets, no privacy
  - Seasickness was near-universal
  - Food was basic: bread, pasta, broth — often spoiled
  - Ventilation was poor; illness spread rapidly
- **Ships:** Italian lines like Lloyd Italiano, Navigazione Generale Italiana; also German and British lines
- **Cost:** ~$25–30 for steerage (a significant sum for peasant families)

### Arrival at Ellis Island
- **First sight:** The Statue of Liberty emerging from harbor mist — a moment of overwhelming emotion documented in countless oral histories
- **Disembarkation:** Passengers lined up on deck as the ship docked; steerage passengers processed last
- **The Process:**
  - Medical inspection at the dock (the "six-second medical" — doctors watched for obvious signs of illness)
  - Registry Room (Great Hall): lined up by nationality for processing
  - Legal inspection: name, origin, destination, occupation, literacy, financial status
  - **The dreaded eye exam:** Hook check for trachoma — using a buttonhook to flip the eyelid; this was the most feared inspection
  - **Detention risk:** Any flagged condition could mean detention, hospitalization, or deportation
  - Average processing time: 3–5 hours; some detained for days or weeks
- **"Island of Tears":** The Italian name for Ellis Island — "L'Isola delle Lacrime" — because of families separated by medical or legal rejection

## Daily Life After Ellis Island

### Settlement Patterns
- **Little Italy (Manhattan):** Centered on Mulberry Street, expanding from Canal to Bleecker
- **Other enclaves:** East Harlem (Italian Harlem), Brooklyn (Williamsburg, Carroll Gardens), Bronx (Belmont / Arthur Avenue)
- **Chain migration:** One family member arrived, found work and housing, then sent money/information for others to follow
- **Padrone system:** Labor brokers (padroni) connected new arrivals with jobs — often exploitative, but essential for those who spoke no English

### Work & Occupations
- **Manual labor:** Construction, dock work, street cleaning, railroad building
- **Skilled trades:** Stonecutters, tailors, barbers, shoemakers (more common among northern Italians)
- **Street vendors:** Selling fruit, vegetables, roasted nuts, ices — iconic Italian immigrant occupation
- **Domestic work:** Women as laundresses, seamstresses, domestic servants
- **Children:** Often worked from age 10–12; child labor was common and legal in this era
- **Subcontracting:** Many Italians were hired through padroni for large infrastructure projects (subways, bridges, buildings)

### Housing
- **Tenements:** Crowded, poorly ventilated, often unsanitary multi-family buildings
  - 4–6 families per floor, shared toilets in hallways
  - Rooms were small (often one room for an entire family)
  - Little Italy tenements on Mulberry Bend were among the worst in the city
- **Rents:** $8–12/month for a small apartment (a significant portion of wages)
- **Overcrowding:** Italian families often larger than other immigrant groups; rooms packed with multiple generations

### Food & Customs
- **Italian food culture persisted:** Pasta, bread, olive oil, wine were staples — imported or made locally
- **Street food culture:** Roasted chestnuts, eels, snails, pizza (sold by street vendors, not yet in restaurants)
- **Coffee:** Italian-style espresso available in Italian cafes
- **Sunday dinner:** The sacred family meal — even in poverty, Sunday pasta was maintained
- **Religious festivals:** Feast of San Gennaro (Little Italy), processions, street altars
- **Wine:** Homemade wine was common; grapes shipped from California or grown locally
- **Markets:** Italian vendors at pushcart markets selling familiar vegetables (artichokes, eggplant, peppers unfamiliar to other groups)

### Social Organization
- **Mutual aid societies:** Società di mutuo soccorso — provided insurance, funeral benefits, community support
- **Regional clubs:** Organized by hometown (paese) — e.g., Club of Calabrians, Sicilian Society
- **Church:** Catholic churches served as community anchors; Italian priests sometimes faced tensions with Irish-dominated parish structures
- **Family structure:** Patriarchal but with strong maternal influence; children's obedience was paramount
- **Music & entertainment:** Street musicians (organ grinders were stereotyped but real), mandolin players, folk songs

## Key Visual & Sensory Details for Screenwriting

### Clothing
- Women: Dark skirts, white or dark blouses, shawls (fazzoletto), simple shoes or boots, hair pinned up
- Men: Work clothes — dark trousers, suspenders, collarless shirts, caps (berretti), sometimes vests
- Children: Hand-me-downs, often oversized; thin coats inadequate for NYC winters (visible in Hine photos)
- **Luggage:** Bundles wrapped in cloth (fagotti), cardboard suitcases, string-tied parcels — not proper luggage

### Language & Communication
- **Italian dialects, not standard Italian:** Sicilian, Calabrese, Neapolitan — these are distinct languages, not just accents
- **Little English:** Most arrivals knew no English; communication at Ellis Island was through interpreters (often fellow Italians working for the Immigration Service)
- **Gestures:** Italian hand gestures and expressive body language were cultural markers

### Emotional Landscape
- **Fear:** Of deportation, of separation from family, of the unknown
- **Hope:** Religious faith, belief in American opportunity, letters from relatives who'd already made it
- **Pride:** Even in poverty, dignity was maintained — the famous Hine photos show exhaustion but not defeat
- **Disorientation:** Noise, crowds, unfamiliar architecture, different food, strange language
- **Community:** Immediate bonding with fellow Italians on the ship and at Ellis Island — strangers becoming instant family
