# Estimated Winter Weather Conditions — New York, circa January 1905

## Sources & Methodology

- NOAA/NCEI Climate Data Online archives (station: NYC Central Park, established 1869)
- ExtremeWeatherWatch.com year-1905 records for NYC
- NYSAES (Cornell) historical weather station data for 1905
- General historical climate literature on the northeastern U.S. in the early 1900s

## January 1905 — General Conditions

### Temperature

- **Average High:** ~35–38°F (1.7–3.3°C)
- **Average Low:** ~22–26°F (−5.6 to −3.3°C)
- **Typical Daily Range:** Cold mornings, modest afternoon warming
- **Extreme Cold Snaps:** Periods dipping to 10°F or below were not uncommon
- **Year 1905 Overall:** The highest recorded NYC temperature in 1905 was 96°F (summer); winters were typical for the era — the "Little Ice Age" influence was fading but winters remained harsher than modern norms

### Snowfall & Precipitation

- **Average January Snowfall:** ~8–12 inches total for the month
- **Typical Snow Events:** 2–3 notable snowstorms per January, with occasional heavier events (6+ inches in a single storm)
- **Snow on Ground:** Likely persistent snow cover through most of January; streets would be slushy, muddy, or icy
- **Rain:** Mixed precipitation common — rain, sleet, and snow alternating
- **Harbor Conditions:** New York Harbor could have ice floes; ferry crossings were cold and wet

### Wind

- **Prevailing Winds:** Northwest to west, bringing cold continental air
- **Gusts:** Frequent, especially along the waterfront and harbor
- **Wind Chill:** Effective temperatures often 10–15°F lower than actual, making outdoor waiting (as at Ellis Island) brutally cold

### Visibility & Atmosphere

- **Fog/Mist:** Common in early mornings over the harbor — ships would emerge from fog banks
- **Haze:** Coal smoke from thousands of furnaces created persistent urban haze (no clean-air regulations yet)
- **Sky:** Frequently overcast; clear, crisp days were memorable exceptions
- **Ice:** The Hudson and East Rivers could have significant ice near shores; small boats sometimes struggled

## Screenwriting Implications

1. **Cold as a Character:** Immigrants arriving from southern Italy (where January temps are 40–55°F) would be shocked by the cold. Their thin clothing, lack of proper winter gear, would be immediately visible and visceral.

2. **Harbor Fog:** Ships arriving through fog banks — the Statue of Liberty emerging from mist — is a documented historical experience and a stunning cinematic image.

3. **Urban Haze:** The coal-smoke haze over Manhattan would give the skyline a sepia-toned, almost dreamlike quality — matching the emotional state of newcomers.

4. **Ice on the Water:** Ice floes in the harbor would make the ferry crossing feel precarious and dramatic.

5. **Wet Cold vs. Dry Cold:** NYC's humidity makes cold feel wetter, more penetrating than Italy's dry cold — immigrants would comment on the difference.

6. **Clothing Details:** Italian immigrants typically wore layered but thin garments — wool shawls, simple coats, no gloves or hats designed for this climate. Children in the famous Lewis Hine photos show inadequate winter clothing.
