# Ellis Island Inspection Process, circa 1905

## Source: History.com & NPS.gov Research

### Overview

From 1900 to 1914 — the peak years of Ellis Island's operation — an average of **1,900 people** passed through the immigration station every day. Most successfully passed through in a matter of hours, but others could be detained for days or weeks.

### Step-by-Step Processing

#### 1. Arrival & Ferry

- Steamship anchors in New York Harbor
- Immigrants transferred to Ellis Island via government barge/ferry
- First view of the island: a cluster of brick buildings with the main Registry Room dominating the landscape

#### 2. Baggage Drop

- Immigrants left their baggage in a large room on the ground floor
- Trunks, bundles, baskets — everything they'd carried across the Atlantic

#### 3. The Staircase

- Immigrants climbed a steep staircase to reach the Registry Room
- **This was the first medical test:** doctors stood at the top watching for signs of lameness, difficulty breathing, or physical weakness
- The staircase became one of the most iconic and feared elements of the Ellis Island experience

#### 4. Medical Inspection

- The "six-second medical" — doctors observed immigrants as they walked past
- Chalk marks on clothing indicated suspected problems:
  - **X** = suspected mental defect
  - **K** = suspected hernia
  - **B** = back problems
  - **E** = eye problems (trachoma was a major concern)
  - **H** = heart problems
  - **Sc** = scalp problems (favus)
- Those marked were sent for detailed examination
- **Trachoma** (a contagious eye disease) was the leading medical cause for deportation

#### 5. Legal Inspection

- Interpreters (who spoke dozens of languages and dialects) helped immigrants answer questions:
  - Name?
  - Age?
  - Occupation?
  - Can you read and write?
  - Destination? Who is waiting for you?
  - How much money do you have?
  - Have you ever been in prison?
  - Are you polygamists?
  - Are you anarchists?
- The 29 questions came from the ship's manifest — inspectors verified the answers matched

#### 6. Detention or Release

- **~98% passed through** and were released within hours
- Detained immigrants waited in dormitory rooms — metal-framed beds, sparse conditions
- Detained immigrants ate in a dining hall — meals were basic American food (bread, stew, coffee)
- Families could be separated if one member was detained — a devastating experience

#### 7. Money Exchange & Ticket Purchase

- Immigrants exchanged foreign currency at teller windows
- Those traveling onward purchased railroad tickets
- Those staying in New York simply walked out to the ferry

#### 8. The Ferry to Manhattan

- Final boat ride from Ellis Island to the Battery (lower Manhattan) or to railroad terminals in Hoboken/Jersey City
- The moment of stepping onto American soil — the culmination of the entire journey

### Key Facts for Screenplay Authenticity

- **Processing time:** 3–7 hours for most; days or weeks for detained cases
- **Rejection rate:** Only ~2%, but that 2% meant thousands of broken dreams
- **Interpreters:** Ellis Island employed interpreters for over 20 languages — including Italian dialects
- **The Great Hall:** A vast, impressive room with high arched ceilings, tiled floors, and enormous windows looking out toward Manhattan
- **The view:** Through those windows, immigrants could see the Manhattan skyline — buildings, smoke, the Brooklyn Bridge — the city they'd dreamed of
- **The noise:** Hundreds of people, crying children, shouted questions, multiple languages — a chaotic, overwhelming environment
- **The fear:** Every immigrant knew that a chalk mark could mean deportation — the terror of being sent back after everything they'd endured
